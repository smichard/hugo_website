This post is a little different from the usual technical content on this blog. I recently finished reading *Entzug* by Christoph Peters, and I wanted to write down a few thoughts about it.

## The Author

Christoph Peters was born in 1966 in Kalkar in the western part of Germany close to the Rhine river. He studied philosophy and painting in Düsseldorf. He has been one of the more quietly significant voices in contemporary German fiction for over two decades, with a body of work that spans literary novels, novellas, and short prose. He lives in Berlin. *Entzug* was published in early 2026 and has drawn considerable attention — and based on my reading, that attention is well deserved.

## What the Book Is About

The novel opens with a bottle of vodka on the kitchen table of a three-person household on a Monday afternoon. That image sets the tone for everything that follows. The narrator is a writer who drinks — initially, as he tells himself, to be able to write and think and feel. At some point, he simply drinks to drink and to suppress his drink-induced tremor. The story traces the arc from that state to its logical endpoint: a choice between continuing and dying, or stopping and living. He chooses to check himself into a clinic.

The book is structured in two parts. The first, *Trinken* (Drinking), documents the daily logistics of late-stage alcoholism — how to obtain alcohol, how to hide it, how to manage the shaking, how to maintain the appearance of a functioning life for family and colleagues, until that appearance can no longer be maintained. The second, *Nicht-Trinken* (Not Drinking), follows the narrator through the clinical process of detox, described with a level of detail that is almost documentary in character.

## My Assessment

What I found most striking about the first part is the language. Peters writes in long, winding sentences that have a quality to them, something slightly unsteady, slightly out of focus, that mirrors the state they are describing. Whether that was a deliberate stylistic choice or my own reading into it, I genuinely cannot say, but the effect is there. Some episodes in this part also carry a tragicomic quality. The elaborate rituals of concealment and the absurd logic of addiction played out in domestic settings make them both darkly funny and quietly devastating at the same time.

The second part shifts register almost completely. The clinical detox is written with a sobriety that matches its subject: flat, precise, procedural. The contrast with the first part is sharp. Again, I am not sure whether Peters made this choice intentionally or whether I am overreading, but it works. The withdrawal scenes, the shaking so severe the narrator cannot hold a fork, the diagnosis of serious liver damage, land with real weight precisely because they are rendered without embellishment. That said, the second part is not without its own tragicomic moments. The clinic scenes, where the narrator observes the other patients checking in with their individual quirks and stories, carry a dry, observational humor that provides some relief without softening what the book is actually about.

The novel is clearly shaped by personal experience. It is difficult to imagine how a writer could describe both the state of heavy intoxication and the process of detox with this level of accuracy and interior detail without having lived through some version of it. How much of the book is autobiographical is something Peters has kept to himself, and in the end it does not matter. What matters is that the book is honest, unsentimental, and does not romanticize what it describes.

*Entzug* is one of the better novels I have read in a while — not an easy read, but a genuinely important one.
